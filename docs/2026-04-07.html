El Financiero México
México aprueba nueva ley de infraestructura para atraer capital privado e institucional de largo plazo
El 25 de marzo, la Cámara de Diputados aprobó la Ley para el Fomento de la Inversión en Infraestructura Estratégica, que rompe con la restricción de suficiencia presupuestal anual y crea un marco transversal para combinar capital público, privado y de AFORES en proyectos con contratos de décadas. El cambio es estructuralmente relevante: el capital global de infraestructura —fondos de pensiones, fondos institucionales— no fluye hacia proyectos donde las reglas cambian cada ejercicio fiscal, y México necesita ese flujo precisamente ahora que la inversión privada está en pausa por la incertidumbre comercial.
La ley llega en un momento crítico: Mexico necesita demostrar certeza jurídica para atraer inversión ante el nearshoring, pero su credibilidad como destino de capital depende de que el marco no sea revertido o reinterpretado en el próximo ciclo político.

Mexico passes new infrastructure law to attract long-term private and institutional capital
On March 25, the Chamber of Deputies approved the Law for the Promotion of Investment in Strategic Infrastructure, breaking with the annual budget sufficiency constraint and creating a cross-sectoral framework to combine public, private, and AFORE capital in projects with decade-long contracts. The change is structurally relevant: global infrastructure capital — pension funds, institutional funds — does not flow toward projects where rules change every fiscal year, and Mexico needs that flow precisely now when private investment is on pause due to commercial uncertainty.
The law arrives at a critical moment: Mexico needs to demonstrate legal certainty to attract nearshoring investment, but its credibility as a capital destination depends on the framework not being reversed or reinterpreted in the next political cycle.

La encuesta refuerza que el rebote de ventas de inmuebles, autos y viajes al exterior es un fenómeno de segmento alto que coexiste con una contracción sostenida del consumo masivo — una bifurcación que complica la narrativa de recuperación económica del gobierno de Milei.

65% of Argentine families can't make ends meet as consumption of basic foods continues to fall
A Pulso Research survey of 1,300 cases reveals that 65% of Argentine families report significant difficulty closing the month, 75% cannot save anything, and two in three were forced to give up additional consumption last month, with beef as the most affected item. The most worrying data point for the market is that 66% of employed workers are very or somewhat concerned about losing their job, a signal that consumer fragility will not be resolved by nominal stabilization but requires real income recovery.
The survey reinforces that the rebound in real estate, auto, and foreign travel sales is an upper-segment phenomenon coexisting with sustained mass consumption contraction — a bifurcation that complicates the Milei government's economic recovery narrative.

El Comité contra la Desaparición Forzada de la ONU (CED) determinó el 2 de abril que existen indicios fundados de que las desapariciones en México constituyen crímenes de lesa humanidad y solicitó escalar el caso a la Asamblea General — una resolución sin precedente de las 2,160 solicitudes que el CED ha recibido desde 2012, de las cuales 819 corresponden a México. Para inversores institucionales con criterios ESG, una escalada formal del caso México a la Asamblea General de la ONU no es un evento puramente político: eleva el perfil de riesgo de gobernanza del país en un momento en que México compite activamente por atraer capital de nearshoring.

UN concludes forced disappearances in Mexico constitute crimes against humanity; government rejects the report
The UN Committee on Enforced Disappearances (CED) determined on April 2 that there are substantiated grounds to conclude that disappearances in Mexico constitute crimes against humanity, and requested escalating the case to the General Assembly — an unprecedented resolution among the 2,160 requests the CED has received since 2012, of which 819 correspond to Mexico. The Sheinbaum government responded by labeling the report 'biased' and 'lacking legal rigor,' a reaction the author equates point by point with those of the Calderón and Peña Nieto governments facing similar humanitarian crises.
For institutional investors with ESG criteria, a formal escalation of the Mexico case to the UN General Assembly is not purely a political event: it raises the country's governance risk profile at a moment when Mexico is actively competing to attract nearshoring capital.
