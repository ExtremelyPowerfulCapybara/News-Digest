Trump trapped in Iran: Brent hits $112 and the Strait of Hormuz remains blocked one month into the war
Nearly one month after the U.S. offensive against Iran began, there has been no quick surrender and no reopening of the Strait of Hormuz — through which 20% of global oil flows — and Brent crude closed Friday at $112, its highest level since July 2022. Iran publicly rejected Trump's 15-point ceasefire plan sent via Pakistan, calling it 'maximalist,' while presenting its own five-point counterproposal that includes war reparations. Barclays estimates a prolonged disruption could remove between 13 and 17 million barrels per day from the market, and Rystad Energy warns that once strategic reserves are depleted — estimated for mid-April — there will be little governments can do to contain prices.

War effect: Argentine bonds fall 2.6% in the month but outperform the emerging-market index; BCRA frees up bank reserve requirements
The weighted average price of Argentine debt has fallen around 2.6% this month, though its relative performance is better than the EMB index adjusted for the high beta of local bonds — the market validates that positive effects on the current account outweigh financial deterioration in the short term. The seasonal dynamics of the grain harvest promise dollar purchases over the next three months, supporting the position. Meanwhile, the BCRA declined to renew a key regulation on bank reserve requirements, freeing up 5 percentage points currently held in public securities — a signal that the economic team is seeking to accelerate compression of lending rates and stimulate credit.

Argentina's K-shaped recovery: agribusiness, mining, and energy grow while the urban economy bears the adjustment
Argentina's economic recovery is not following a uniform trajectory but a K-shape: some sectors are growing rapidly — agribusiness driven by global food demand, mining by critical minerals, energy by the closure of Hormuz — while others are registering closures and layoffs. The absence of domestic currency and credit makes it harder to cushion the productive transition, unlike other economies in similar situations. The La Nacion editorial argues that Argentina, unlike in the past, no longer has time for a gradual adaptation given the surge of artificial intelligence and Chinese competition.

Corte Suprema propone limitar la discrecionalidad política en la selección de magistrados en Argentina

Argentina's Supreme Court proposes curbing political discretion in judicial selection
Justices Carlos Rosenkrantz and Ricardo Lorenzetti submitted a proposal to the Magistrates' Council to reduce political influence in the selection of judges, capping the weight of personal interviews at 10% of the total score and separating those who set exam questions from those who grade them. Constitutional lawyers including Ricardo Gil Lavedra and Alberto Garay welcomed the initiative as a 'major contribution' to judicial independence, noting that interviews have historically led to 'suspicious alterations of merit rankings.' The proposal must be adopted by the full Council to take effect, and its political path forward will be the real test of its reach.
